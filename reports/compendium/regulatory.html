Open banking initiatives by country


Regulators are increasingly moving towards open account access, and, in some countries, payment initiation.

In 2015, the European Parliament passed the devised Directive on Payment Services, known as PSD2. It was implemented in January 2018, with a final compliance deadline having passed in September 2019. Under PSD2, European regulators award licenses to Third Party Payment providers (TPP) allowing them to get direct access to clients’ bank accounts via an API. Through the API, TTPs are allowed to perform two functions:

  • Account Information Services (AIS) which is the ability to aggregate bank account and transaction information for customers on a single platform (i.e. “read” access);
  • Payment Initiation Services (PIS) which is the ability to initiate payment transactions at the request of the user from an account held by the user at any other payment services provider (i.e. “write” access).

    • Regulations in the UK take open banking even further than PSD". In 2016 the UK Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) required the nine largest banks in the UK to adopt “open API banking standards… [and] to make data available using these standards.” Other banks can opt-in on a voluntary basis. The goal of the initiative was to increase competition, including lowering costs for consumers to switch between financial institutions. Thus, as well as incorporating the core features of PSD2, it additionally requires the inclusion of publicly available information on branch and ATM locations, bank products and fees. In 2016, the CMA established the Open Banking Implementation Entity to instance the adoption of open banking in UK financial services. The body’s role is to design API specifications, support banks and third-party providers, create security standards, manage the Open Banking directory (including enrolling third-party providers) and create standards for managing disputes and complaints.

    Banking-as-a-service


    Banking-as-a-service is nothing new. Banks have white-labelled banking services for others, principally retailers, for many years. One of the highest profile BaaS clients in the Germany is the Post Office, which has used Postbank since 1909 (initially called Postcheckdienst, by establishing accounts for payment transactions by mail and linking postal and banking services in German states). It has later been sold to the Deutsche Bank as a result of a demerger of the postal savings division of Deutsche Bundespost in 1990. The Post Office is responsible for sales performance and marketing, while Postbank i.e. Deutsche Bank is responsible for product development, pricing and service delivery.

    Nevertheless, technology embedded within APIs makes it a lot easier for banks to white label their core functionality. More than simple data sharing – a feature of open banking – banking-as-a-service offers banking services to third-parties. Customers can be other banks white labelling the BaaS platform for a faster time to market, fintech firms leveraging the BaaS provider’s banking license, or banks and fintechs using the BaaS platform for testing purposes.
